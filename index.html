Autism Spectrum Disorder

DSM‑5 Criteria · Early Signs · Nursing Care | Adele Pillitteri, 7e

Definition & Epidemiology

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by persistent deficits in social communication/interaction and restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities. Symptoms present in early childhood and limit daily functioning.

📊 Global incidence
~1 in 36 children (CDC, 2023). More common in boys (4:1 ratio). In the Philippines, prevalence estimated 1 in 100–150, with increasing recognition.
🧠 Key concept
ASD is a “spectrum” – wide variation in abilities and needs. No two individuals are alike.
⚠️ Early identification is critical: Early intervention improves outcomes.

Etiology & Risk Factors

⚠️ Risk Factors
• Advanced parental age
• Prematurity / low birth weight
• Genetic syndromes (Fragile X, tuberous sclerosis, Rett)
• Family history of ASD
• Prenatal exposure to valproate, alcohol
• Maternal obesity/diabetes
🧬 Etiology
Strong genetic component (heritability ~80%). Multiple genes involved; no single cause. Environmental factors may interact with genetic predisposition. Vaccines DO NOT cause ASD (extensive evidence).
📌 Myth vs Fact: Thimerosal (vaccine preservative) does NOT cause autism. Numerous studies have refuted the link.

Clinical Features (DSM‑5 Criteria)

A. Social Communication deficits
• Poor eye contact, lack of joint attention
• Delayed or absent language
• Difficulty with back‑and‑forth conversation
• Reduced sharing of emotions/interests
• Difficulty understanding social cues
B. Restricted, repetitive behaviors
• Stereotyped movements (hand flapping, rocking)
• Insistence on sameness, rigidity
• Intense fixated interests (e.g., trains, numbers)
• Hyper‑ or hyporeactivity to sensory input
Early red flags (M‑CHAT): No babbling by 12 months, no gestures by 12 months, no single words by 16 months, loss of language/skills at any age.

Screening & Diagnosis

  • Screening tools: M‑CHAT (Modified Checklist for Autism in Toddlers) at 18 and 24 months. Positive screen → further evaluation.
  • Comprehensive diagnostic evaluation: Developmental pediatrician, child psychologist, or child psychiatrist. ADOS‑2 (Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule) is gold standard.
  • Multidisciplinary team: Speech therapy, occupational therapy, psychology, neurology.
  • Rule out other conditions: Hearing test, lead screening, genetic testing (Fragile X, microarray).
Nursing role: Administer M‑CHAT, educate parents about milestones, refer for early intervention if concerns arise.

Management & Therapies

🧩 Behavioral & Educational
• Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) – evidence‑based
• Speech‑language therapy
• Occupational therapy (sensory integration)
• Social skills training
• Individualized Education Program (IEP)
💊 Medications (for co‑occurring symptoms)
• Risperidone, aripiprazole – for irritability/aggression
• SSRIs – for anxiety, repetitive behaviors
• Stimulants – for ADHD symptoms
• Melatonin – for sleep disturbances
Early Intensive Behavioral Intervention (EIBI): Starting before age 3 improves IQ, language, adaptive skills.

Nursing Care & Family Support

  • Support parents: Provide resources (support groups, respite care). Acknowledge parental stress and grief. Encourage self‑care.
  • Communication strategies: Use simple, concrete language; visual schedules; picture exchange communication system (PECS); allow extra response time.
  • Sensory considerations: Reduce environmental stimuli (dim lights, quiet area); provide fidget toys, weighted blankets; respect sensory aversions.
  • Sibling support: Explain autism, address feelings of jealousy or embarrassment.
  • Transition planning: Prepare for school changes, adolescence, adulthood (vocational training, independent living).
Mnemonic “C.A.R.E.” – Communication strategies, Adjust environment, Respect routines, Empathize with family.

Prognosis & Long‑Term Outcomes

  • Early intensive intervention significantly improves language, social skills, and adaptive functioning.
  • Some individuals become independent, others require lifelong support.
  • Associated conditions: intellectual disability (30‑50%), epilepsy (10‑30%), anxiety, depression, ADHD, GI issues.
  • Many adults with ASD can work and live semi‑independently with supports.
🌟 Positive outlook: With appropriate support, individuals with ASD can lead fulfilling lives and contribute to society.
